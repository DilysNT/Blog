Diagnosing Your Symptoms: What Tooth Sensitivity After Filling Tells Us

Dr. Steven Nguyen
‚Ä¢ Dec 23, 2025 ‚Ä¢ 5 min read

When you sit in my chair complaining of pain a few days after a procedure, my first job isn't to guess; it is to diagnose based on the specific type of pain you are describing. Pain is a language. The way your tooth hurts‚Äîwhen it hurts, how long it lasts, and what triggers it‚Äîtells me exactly what is happening underneath the restoration.

We don't just treat "pain"; we treat the specific mechanical or biological cause. In clinical practice, tooth sensitivity after filling presents in very distinct patterns. Let's break down the common symptom profiles I see in daily practice and what they usually indicate regarding the success of the restoration.

Scenario 1: The Sharp Zing on Biting (Occlusal Trauma)

This is perhaps the most common phone call I get two days after a procedure. You feel perfectly fine when you're drinking coffee or sitting still. But the moment you chew a piece of chicken or lettuce, you get a sharp, electric shock sensation.

This pattern almost always means the filling is "high." When you were numb, you couldn't feel your bite accurately. If the new filling is even a fraction of a millimeter taller than your other teeth, it hits first. This bruises the ligament holding the tooth in the jawbone. We solve this by marking the high spot and polishing it down. The relief is usually instant.

Scenario 2: The Pain When You Let Go (Bond Failure)

This symptom is subtle but distinct. You bite down, and it feels okay. But the moment you open your mouth or release the pressure from the bite, you feel a sharp pain.

üî¨ The Clinical Translation

This suggests a failure in the adhesive layer, often called the "hydrodynamic vacuum." If the filling didn't bond perfectly, a microscopic gap exists. When you bite, you compress the gap. When you release, suction pulls fluid through the tooth structure, triggering the nerve. Unfortunately, the filling usually needs to be replaced to re-establish a sealed bond.

Scenario 3: Cold Sensitivity That Vanishes Quickly

You take a sip of ice water, and it hurts. But the moment you swallow the water, the pain disappears within five to ten seconds.

This is "Reversible Pulpitis." It means the nerve is annoyed but alive (a bruised nerve). The drilling and acid etching have irritated the pulp. This is very common. Unless it gets worse, my advice is to wait and give the "bruise" time to fade before intervening.

Scenario 4: The Throbbing Ache (Irreversible Symptoms)

This is the scenario that concerns us. You aren't eating or drinking anything, and the tooth starts to throb. Or, the pain wakes you up at night. Or, cold water makes it hurt for a full minute after the cold is gone.

This tells me the nerve has sustained too much damage‚Äîlikely from deep decay‚Äîand cannot heal. The inflammation is self-perpetuating. In this case, a filling adjustment won't help. We likely need to discuss root canal therapy to stop the pain.

Conclusion: Not all pain is the same. By paying close attention to the specific triggers‚Äîpressure, release, temperature, or spontaneity‚Äîwe can pinpoint the source of the problem. If it hurts only when you bite, come back for an adjustment. If it hurts spontaneously, call us immediately.
